Analysis tools for characterizing tissue surfaces of any shape

How are cells aligned on tissue surfaces? Does the alignment depend on local curvature? Where are regions of misalignment? Do these regions correlate spatially with the fluorescence signal of the molecular marker of interest? And how do all these properties change over time?

These are challenging questions that are not easy to answer, as we are capturing two-dimensional images of three-dimensional (3D) tissues. The main difficulty lies in accurately stitching together individual images from a z-stack and reconstructing 3D information, independent of the shape and structure of the multicellular system. We took on this challenge and developed an image analysis pipeline to fully capture tissue properties such as the nematic order, topological defects, local curvature, and fluorescence signals from molecular markers across the entire surface of 3D tissues.

Capturing these 3D properties enables us, e.g., to verify theoretical predictions from simulations by investigating the nematic orientation field of surface cells in multicellular aggregates, as well as to link biological mechanisms with physical descriptions in zebrafish heart systems. Our image analysis tools now make it possible to quantitatively analyze tissue surfaces and help us address the questions outlined above.

Nematic and hexatic order in tissues

Cytoskeleton filaments, bacteria, and various cell types often exhibit symmetries such as polar order (symmetry under rotations by 360°) and nematic order (symmetry under rotations by 180°). The latter is one of the most commonly used symmetries in describing tissues. Elongated cells can be modeled as rod-like objects that align collectively, forming so-called liquid crystal phases—specifically nematic phases, which are characterized by long-range orientational order without positional order.

Nematic order, however, requires elongated cells. Are cells really elongated? Not always! In many tissues, such as epithelia, the cells are organized in honeycomb-like lattices, where most cells are hexagonal, indicating hexatic order (symmetry under rotations by 60°). This raises an important question: Are tissues better described as nematic or hexatic?

To address this question, we combined in vitro experiments with numerical simulations to analyze the symmetry of epithelial tissues. Unexpectedly, we discovered that epithelial tissues support multiple types of liquid crystal orders that co-exist at multiple length scales: they are predominantly hexatic at small scales, e.g., as single cells or with their neighbors, and predominantly nematic at large scales, e.g., from the perspective of the entire tissue. We also found that the hexanematic crossover—the length scale at which the switch occurs—is not a fixed value. It depends significantly on monolayer density, cell-cell adhesion, and the underlying substrate stiffness. We identified the crossover length scale as a robust indicator for changes in mechanics and biochemistry that influence tissue integrity. Therefore, exploring the crossover scale between hexatic and nematic symmetry allows for a deeper understanding of the cellular organization of tissues during development, homeostasis, and disease.
